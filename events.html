How Did Badminton Start?

Badminton may look like a modern, fast-paced sport, but its roots stretch back hundreds of years through lawn games, children’s toys, and a game called Poona played by British officers in India. Over time, those simple rallies evolved into the Olympic sport we know today.

Early shuttle-and-racket games were played in Europe and Asia long before the word “badminton” existed. Simple wooden rackets and feathered shuttles were used in garden games and parlour sports.

Today’s rackets use carbon fibre and advanced design to handle smashes that can exceed 400 km/h. The sport has transformed from a casual pastime into a professional, highly physical game.

From Poona to the Olympic Stage

19th Century Origins

British officers in India played a game called Poona, using rackets to hit a shuttlecock over a line. When they brought the game back to England, it was famously played at the Duke of Beaufort’s estate—Badminton House—giving the sport its modern name.

Rules and Clubs

In the late 1800s, clubs in England began writing official rules and organizing competitions. The Badminton Association of England was founded in 1893 and the first All England Championships were held in 1899, quickly becoming the sport’s most prestigious tournament.

A Global Power Sport

In the 20th century, badminton spread across Asia and Europe. Nations such as Indonesia, China, Korea, Denmark, and Malaysia turned it into a high-level competitive sport, leading to world championships and its eventual inclusion in the Olympic Games.
